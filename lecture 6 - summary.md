## Part 1: Ovid's Metamorphoses and the Ages of Man

Ovid’s account of the ages of man explores the gradual worsening of human morality over time.

### Background and Influences

* Ovid wrote in Rome approximately 700 years after Hesiod.


* The myth of the ages of man was not invented by Hesiod; it was an older tradition that he inherited and recorded.


* Ovid's literary predecessors also wrote about this tradition, including the Hellenistic poet Aratus in his didactic epic *Phenomena*.


* The Roman poet Virgil also addressed this in his pastoral *Bucolics* (or *Eclogues*), specifically in the "Messianic Eclogue," which prophesied the birth of a boy who would bring about the return of the Golden Age.



### The Creation of Humanity

Before detailing the ages, Ovid explores the unique descent of humanity, suggesting humans are finer beings capable of mind and ruling.

* 
**Divine Image:** Man may have been created in God's image.


* 
**Stoic Inspiration:** Humans might have been formed from newly separated earth that still retained celestial force or divine seeds.


* 
**Divine Spark:** This aligns with Stoic philosophy, which argues there is a divine spark in everything, but it exists in the highest quantity within humanity.


* 
**Prometheus:** Alternatively, Prometheus (the "lover of man") may have created humanity. A fable suggests that to balance the number of humans and animals, Prometheus transformed beasts into people, explaining why some humans have a beastly character.



### The Golden Age

The most distinct difference between Ovid and Hesiod is that Ovid strictly follows the degeneration of metals and completely omits the generation of heroes. The Golden Age is depicted as an era of absolute peace and natural abundance.

* 
**Justice and Law:** People naturally cherished justice and right without the need for laws, punishments, or fear.


* 
**No Written Laws:** There were no "bronze tablets" (a reference to the 12 tablets of Roman law) or judges.


* 
**Peace and Isolation:** Trees were not cut down to build ships, meaning people stayed in their own territories without wandering.


* 
**No War:** Towns lacked defensive moats and walls, and there were no trumpets, swords, helmets, or soldiers.


* 
**Natural Bounty:** The earth was not harried by plows or agriculture; it freely provided berries, cherries, and acorns.


* 
**Eternal Spring:** The climate featured an eternal spring with rivers of milk, rivers of honey, and golden nectar dripping from oak trees.



### Transition to the Silver Age

The Golden Age ended when Saturn (the Roman equivalent of the Greek Titan Cronos) was defeated in a cosmic war against the younger gods led by Jove (Jupiter/Zeus) and driven into Tartarus, the shadowy underworld. This conflict caused a degeneration in morality.

* 
**Climate Change:** Jove shortened spring and introduced winter, summer, and autumn.


* 
**Harsh Conditions:** The air glowed white-hot in summer, and icicles formed in winter, forcing humanity to build houses instead of living in open thickets.


* 
**Agriculture and Labor:** Seeds had to be planted in furrows, and oxen suffered and groaned under the heavy yoke of farm labor.

## Part 2: The Bronze and Iron Ages

As time progressed, the degradation of metals continued to symbolize moral decline.

### The Bronze Age

* 
**Aggression:** Human dispositions developed aggressive instincts.


* 
**First Wars:** People were quick to arm themselves and initiated all-out war, though they were not yet entirely evil.



### The Iron Age

The Iron Age represents the complete collapse of morality and is framed as the stark opposite of the Golden Age.

* 
**Loss of Virtues:** Modesty, truth, and righteousness fled the earth.


* 
**Rise of Vices:** These virtues were replaced by trickery, slyness, violence, and the "damned desire of having".


* 
**Exploration and Boundaries:** Trees were cut down for ships to sail unknown winds, and surveyors drew borders on land that was once free to everyone.


* 
**Mining:** Humans dug into the earth's "vitals" and Stygian shadows to extract the "root of evil": iron and gold.


* 
**Warfare and Greed:** War was personified, using both iron for weapons and gold as a motive to fight.


* 
**Family Violence:** Strife infected homes; guests were unsafe from hosts, siblings fought, spouses murdered one another, stepmothers brewed aconite poison, and sons wished for their fathers' deaths to claim their inheritance.


* 
**The Departure of Justice:** Piety was vanquished, and Astrea, the maiden goddess of Justice, became the last immortal to flee the blood-soaked earth.

## Part 3: Herodotus and the Birth of History

The lecture transitions from 1st-century Roman poetry to 5th-century BC Greek prose.  Herodotus, famously dubbed the "father of history" by Cicero, wrote a monumental prose work called *The Histories*.

### Background and Context

* 
**Life:** Born around 485 BC in Halicarnassus (modern-day Turkey), he traveled extensively to Athens, Egypt, and Babylon, before settling and dying in Thurii, South Italy around 425 BC.


* 
**Language and Distribution:** Though he recited his works in Athens, he wrote in the Ionic dialect of Greek. The origin of history was rooted in oral communication because books were rare, expensive, and literacy was low.


* 
**Predecessors:** He was preceded by logographers (story writers) and geographers like Hecataeus of Miletus, who drew early maps and wrote of navigating the known world. Miletus was considered the cradle of Greek science, producing thinkers like Thales.


* 
**Structure:** *The Histories* spans up to 800 pages in modern editions and was divided into nine books named after the nine Muses by later Hellenistic scholars in Alexandria.



### Methodology

* 
**Research:** The Greek word *historia* means "research".


* 
**Sources:** Without libraries, Herodotus relied on what he saw (eyesight) and what he heard (hearsay).


* 
**Judgment:** He applied scientific methods by exercising judgment, comparing conflicting accounts, and recording what he considered most reliable.



### Literary Influences

Herodotus was heavily shaped by the Greek literary tradition:

* 
**Epic Poetry (Homer):** Influenced his choice of a monumental subject (a massive conflict), heroic personalities (like Leonidas), and the use of lengthy speeches before battle scenes.


* 
**Wisdom Literature:** Evident in scenes focused on instruction, using question-and-answer formats (e.g., the story of Solon and Croesus asking "who is the happiest?").


* 
**Tragedy:** The greatest influence, emphasizing two key themes:


* 
*Belated Understanding:* Characters (like King Croesus) realizing their fatal mistakes only after it is too late.


* 
*Balanced Order:* The belief that there is no chaos in the universe; everything resolves according to a balanced order, fate, or necessity, often resulting in retribution for wrongs committed.

## Part 4: The Preface and Rationalizing Myth

*The Histories* focuses on the massive conflict between the East (Persia) and the West (Greece). The recommended modern English translation for studying this text is "The Landmark Herodotus," translated by Andrea L. Purvis.

### The Proemium (Preface)

* Unlike epic poets, Herodotus does not invoke a Muse to assist his writing.


* His stated purpose is to ensure that human events and the great deeds of both the Hellenes (Greeks) and Barbarians (non-Greek speakers) do not fade from memory with time.


* He also aims to document the specific causes that led them to war.



### Rationalizing Myth: The Cycle of Abductions

In the first five chapters, Herodotus rationalizes ancient Greek myths by stripping away magic and monsters, explaining the East-West conflict as an escalating cycle of abducted women.

* 
**Incident 1 (The East acts):** Phoenician merchants arrived in Argos (Greece) and abducted the princess Io, taking her to Egypt. This is a rationalized version of the myth where Io is turned into a cow.


* 
**Incident 2 (The West retaliates):** Hellenes (Cretans) sailed to Tyre in Phoenicia and abducted the king's daughter, Europa, making the score even.


* 
**Incident 3 (The West acts again):** The Greeks (Argonauts) sailed to Colchis and abducted Medea. When her father demanded her return and compensation, the Greeks refused, citing the unresolved abduction of Io. The score became uneven.


* 
**Incident 4 (The East retaliates):** Paris (Alexander) of Troy abducted Helen of Sparta, expecting no penalty because the Greeks had not paid for Medea.



### The Escalation to War

Instead of continuing the cycle of reciprocal abductions, the Greeks launched a massive military expedition—the Trojan War. According to Persian authorities, the Greeks were largely responsible for the major offenses because they made war on Asia before Asia made war on Europe. The Trojan War served as the forerunner to the later Persian invasions.
