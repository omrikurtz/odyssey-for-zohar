### Part 1: Introduction to Herodotus and the Birth of History

**Course Adjustments & Context**

* The remaining third of the semester will focus more heavily on Virgil's *Aeneid*, prioritizing an in-depth reading over covering a larger quantity of works superficially. 


* This deeper dive into Virgil will come at the expense of studying the *Argonautica*. 



**Herodotus and His Predecessors**

* Herodotus is famously known as the "father of history" (a title given by Cicero), though critics like Plutarch have called him the "greatest of liars" for supposedly misleading his readers. 


* Prior to Herodotus, there were no historians or libraries; he effectively created the discipline of historiography *ab ovo* (from the very beginning). 


* His predecessors were "logographers" (writers of stories/myths) and geographers, who focused on mythological tales and maps rather than preserving the memory of recent events using scientific tools. 


* Herodotus originated from Halicarnassus in Ionia, traveled widely (including Egypt, Babylon, and South Italy), and gathered information based on actual reports from wise men. 



**Three Major Influences on Herodotus**

* 
**Epic Poetry:** Epic influenced the grand scale of Herodotus's subject matter (a "world war" between East and West) and its oral presentation, as his prose was designed to be recited to an audience. 


* 
**Folklore and Wisdom Literature:** This is evident in the inclusion of tales featuring wise men instructing rulers on how to live correctly and achieve happiness. 


* 
**Tragedy:** Herodotus viewed history through a tragic lens, believing that a constant state of cosmic balance exists between good and evil. 



**Historical Setting**

* Herodotus aimed to research (*historiin*) the causes of the great conflict between the Greeks and Persians. 


* The Persian invasion ended in defeat around 479-478 BC at the Battle of Plataea. 


* This victory ushered in the 50-year heyday of classical Athens (478 BC to 431 BC), a period of great prosperity that eventually waned with the outbreak of the Peloponnesian War.

### Part 2: Rationalizing Myth and the Cosmic Balance

**Mundane Explanations for Mythical Conflicts**

* Herodotus sought to explain the historical animosity between East and West in a rational, "mundane" way, stripping away supernatural elements. 


* He attributes the root of the conflict to a cycle of mutual abductions of women (driven by trade and lust), rather than divine intervention. 


* According to Persian authorities cited by Herodotus, Io was abducted by Phoenician merchants, contrary to the myth that she was turned into a cow. 


* The Greeks retaliated by abducting Europa and Medea, with Herodotus notably omitting any mention of Medea's magical powers or the miraculous Golden Fleece. 


* Paris (Alexandros) later abducted Helen to settle the score, confident he would face no penalty since the Greeks had not paid compensation for Medea. 


* The East viewed the subsequent Trojan War as a massive overreaction by the Greeks, asserting that sensible people should not make a fuss over abducted women. 



**The Concept of Ananke (Necessity)**

* When Herodotus mentions that "God" is envious of successful people, he is referring to the concept of *Ananke* (necessity), a tragic cosmic force that enforces balance and retribution. 


* This principle dictates that one cannot be excessively wealthy or successful without eventually facing a downfall to restore the universe's equilibrium. 



**The Scope of History**

* Because the mythological accounts were too remote to verify, Herodotus begins his solid historical record with King Croesus of Lydia in the 6th century BC, the first man known to begin unjust acts against the Hellenes. 


* Herodotus dedicates his work to both lesser and greater cities because he holds the knowledge that human prosperity never remains constant and is always in a state of flux. 


* To explain Croesus's genealogy, Herodotus references a tale where King Candaules forced his officer Gyges to view the queen naked; the queen then conspired with Gyges to murder Candaules, bringing a curse of retribution upon their descendant, Croesus, five generations later.

### Part 3: The Story of Solon and Croesus

**The Encounter at Sardis**

* Solon, an Athenian lawgiver who had established binding social reforms, left Athens for 10 years and visited the court of King Croesus in Sardis, Lydia. 


* Hoping to be validated as the happiest of men due to his immense wealth, Croesus gives Solon a tour of his treasuries and asks who is the most prosperous person Solon has ever seen. 



**Solon's True Definition of Happiness**

* Solon shocks Croesus by awarding first place to Tellus of Athens. 


* Tellus was chosen because he lived in a famous city, saw his children and grandchildren survive him, was moderately well-off, and died nobly on the battlefield defending his city. 


* For second place, Solon names Cleobis and Biton, two Argive youths who demonstrated immense filial devotion by pulling their mother's cart five miles to the temple of Hera. 


* After their mother prayed for them to receive the best thing a human could have, the youths fell asleep in the sanctuary and died at the absolute peak of their glory. 


* Solon explains that human life is pure chance, and no single day is like another; therefore, no one can be called truly happy until they have ended their life well. 


* According to Solon, a wealthy man who is currently prosperous should only be called "lucky," not happy, until his final fate is known. 



**Croesus's Downfall and Realization**

* Croesus arrogantly dismisses Solon as a fool for prioritizing the end of a matter over present riches. 


* Shortly after, divine vengeance falls upon Croesus: his son dies tragically, and he misinterprets an Oracle from Delphi, leading him to wage a disastrous war against Persia that destroys his own empire. 


* Captured and placed on a burning pyre by the Persian King Cyrus, Croesus finally understands Solon's wisdom and cries out his name three times. 


* Cyrus learns the meaning behind the outburst and, realizing he too is subject to human fate and retribution, orders Croesus to be saved. 


* The flames are too wild to control, but Croesus prays to Apollo, who miraculously sends a sudden rainstorm to extinguish the fire.

### Part 4: Conclusion

**The Aftermath**

* After being spared, Croesus becomes an advisor to his conqueror, Cyrus. 


* When asked why he waged war instead of seeking friendship, Croesus deflects blame onto the God of the Hellenes (Apollo) for encouraging him. 


* Croesus delivers a profound concluding thought on the tragedy of conflict: "In peace, sons bury fathers, in war, fathers bury sons." 


* The lecture concludes by reiterating that Herodotus's history ultimately demonstrates how balance and order in the universe are maintained by divine will and necessity.
