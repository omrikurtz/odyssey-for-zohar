### Part 1: Background & Literary Context

This section establishes Hesiod's identity, his origins, and his place within ancient Greek literature alongside Homer.

* 
**Hesiod's Identity**: Hesiod is considered the second great ancient Greek poet, following Homer.


* 
**Biographical Details**: Compared to Homer, much more is known about Hesiod's life.


* 
**Family Origin**: His father emigrated from Ionia to mainland Greece.


* 
**Location and Profession**: Hesiod lived in the Boeotian village of Ascra and worked as a shepherd grazing flocks on Mount Helicon.


* 
**Family Conflict**: He had a brother named Perses, to whom he later addressed his work.


* 
**Dating**: Scholars generally agree that Hesiod and Homer were contemporaries who lived and worked in the 8th century BC.


* 
**The Fictitious Contest**: There is a legendary, though historically fictitious, tradition of a poetic contest between them known as the *Certamen Hesiodi et Homeri*.


* 
**Poetic Tradition**: Like Homer, Hesiod's work belongs to the oral tradition.


* 
**Dialect and Meter**: Both poets composed using the artificial Homeric dialect and dactylic hexameter meter.


* 
**Genre Distinction**: While Homer composed heroic epic poetry (focusing on battles and gods on a grand scale), Hesiod established the genre of didactic epic poetry.


* 
**Didactic Poetry**: This genre specifically aims to educate and instruct the audience in a certain field.


* 
**The Epiphany of the Muses**: Hesiod claims that while grazing his sheep, the Muses descended from Mount Helicon, reprimanded shepherds as being "nothing but bellies," and granted him divine inspiration by giving him a laurel staff and breathing a divine voice into him.

### Part 2: The *Works and Days* & Etiology

This section covers Hesiod's undisputed surviving works, focusing heavily on the underlying meaning and personal motivations behind the *Works and Days*.

* 
**Surviving Works**: The two undisputed didactic epic poems attributed to Hesiod are the *Theogony* and the *Works and Days*.


* 
**The Theogony**: An epic poem consisting of 798 lines that describes the genealogy of the gods and the creation of the universe.


* 
**The Works and Days**: An agricultural, didactic poem of 698 lines that explains why humans must toil in the world and how to effectively farm the land.


* 
**Scale**: Both works are significantly shorter than Homer's epics (e.g., the *Iliad* is roughly 16,000 lines).


* 
**Wisdom Literature**: The *Works and Days* functions as wisdom literature, aiming to instruct humanity on right and wrong, similar to the biblical Book of Proverbs.


* 
**Etiology**: A central theme of the poem is etiology, which is the attempt to find and explain the causes of things—in this case, why human life is so hard.


* 
**Personal Motivation**: The *Works and Days* is addressed to Hesiod's brother, Perses.


* 
**The Dispute**: Perses allegedly used corrupt connections with powerful village figures to fraudulently take more than his fair share of their inherited property (patrimony).


* 
**The Lesson**: Hesiod advises Perses that "the half is more than the whole," meaning it is better to possess a smaller portion gained justly than a larger portion gained through fraud.


* 
**Two Kinds of Strife (Eris)**: Hesiod outlines two distinct types of strife.


* 
**Good Strife**: Fosters ambition, emulation, and healthy competition in peaceful times (e.g., working hard to match a neighbor's successful farm).


* 
**Bad Strife**: Leads to violence, war, and attempts to gain possessions by fraudulent means, violating the rules of justice (Dike) overseen by Zeus.

### Part 3: Prometheus, Pandora, and The Ages of Man

This section details the specific etiological myths Hesiod uses to explain the introduction of hardship, labor, and evil into human existence.

**The Prometheus Myth**

* 
**The Deception**: Prometheus ("Forethought") deceived Zeus regarding a sacrifice at Mecone, tricking him into taking the lesser portion.


* 
**The Punishment**: In anger, Zeus concealed fire—representing technology, craft, and convenience—from humanity.


* 
**The Theft**: Prometheus, the son of the Titan Iapetus, acted as a trickster and stole the fire back, hiding it inside a hollow fennel stalk.


* 
**Zeus's Retaliation**: Zeus devised a painful evil for human beings as punishment for embracing the stolen fire.



**The Creation of Pandora**

* 
**Hephaestus**: Ordered by Zeus, this craftsman mixed earth and water to create the beautiful physical form of a maiden.


* 
**Athena**: Taught the maiden crafts, specifically weaving.


* 
**Aphrodite**: Shed grace and "limb-devouring cares" (lust and painful desire) upon her.


* 
**Hermes**: Placed a voice, a "dog's mind," and a "thievish character" into her.


* 
**Personified Deities**: Abstract deities treated as physical beings, such as Persuasion (Peitho) and the Seasons, gifted her golden jewelry.


* 
**The Name**: Hermes named her Pandora ("All-gift") because all the Olympian gods contributed to her creation.


* 
**Epimetheus**: Prometheus's brother, Epimetheus ("Afterthought"), ignored warnings not to accept gifts from Zeus and took Pandora.


* 
**The Jar**: Pandora opened a large jar (not a box), releasing countless miseries, sicknesses, and evils to roam the earth.


* 
**Hope**: Only Hope (Elpis / anticipation) remained trapped inside the jar.



**The Ages of Man**

* 
**Purpose**: This myth explains the moral degradation of humanity over time, comparing generations to descending values of metals.


* 
**The Golden Age**: Created by the Titans while Cronos ruled. Humans lived a paradisiacal existence free from toil and distress, experienced eternal youth, and gathered crops the earth bore freely. Upon dying peacefully, they became guardian spirits walking the earth.


* 
**The Silver Age**: Created by the younger Olympian gods. They experienced a 100-year childhood and a short adulthood plagued by pain due to their acts of folly. They refused to sacrifice to the gods; consequently, Zeus destroyed them, and they became honored spirits beneath the earth.


* 
**The Bronze Age**: Created by Zeus out of ash trees. This terrible and strong race cared only for violence and the "painful works of Ares" (war). They did not eat bread, sustaining themselves otherwise. *(The lecture ends before concluding the final two ages)*.
